The Mission

How do communities keep moving when fuel runs out?

The scenario: a regional conflict has cut off major oil routes across Southeast Asia, causing fuel reserves to drop sharply in Malaysia, Indonesia, Thailand and the Philippines. Petrol stations ration supply, delivery riders can't afford their routes, and the people who rely on them — families, clinics, small businesses — feel it first.

Long queues at petrol stations during fuel shortages — a common sight in crisis scenarios

Flooded roads cut communities off — drones bypass this entirely

Transport Costs Rise

Delivery Delays

Food and essential supplies arrive late or not at all, harming vulnerable communities.

Higher Prices

Families, schools and small businesses feel growing financial pressure on every purchase.

Emergency Services

Ambulances and flood rescue teams struggle to operate at full capacity without fuel.

Our Response

A delivery network that runs on sunlight, not petrol.

DROPPE replaces fuel-burning riders and vans with a fleet of autonomous drones, charged entirely by solar Auto-Swap Stations. Every order — a hot meal, medicine, or an emergency kit — is flown directly to the customer through live-mapped airspace, with zero litres of petrol consumed.

Because it doesn't compete for road space or fuel supply, DROPPE keeps working precisely when the crisis is worst — during rationing, congestion, or when flooded roads cut a community off entirely.

How It Works

From order to landing, in six steps.

01

Order placed in-app

A customer requests a Food Pod or Emergency Pod through the DROPPE app, available in English, 中文, or Bahasa Melayu.

02

Smart Zone System checks the sky

The app maps the area into Safe, Caution, and No-Fly zones before a drone ever leaves the ground — ensuring every flight is legal and safe.

03

The right pod is loaded

A temperature-controlled Food Pod, or an Emergency Pod with a full medical kit, is attached to the drone airframe in seconds.

04

Weather-adaptive flight

Wind, rain and heat are monitored live — the drone adjusts altitude, speed and route automatically for safety in Malaysia's tropical climate.

05

Direct delivery, no roads needed

The drone lands at the customer's door — no traffic, no detours, no petrol burned. GPS-guided precision landing within 1 metre of target.

06

Solar Auto-Swap charging

The drone returns to a solar-powered station, where its battery is swapped or recharged — zero downtime, zero fuel, ready for the next order.

🧭 Empathise — Understanding who is affected

Before we designed anything, we spent time understanding the real people who would be hurt most by a fuel crisis. We identified three primary user groups:

  • Families in flood-prone areas — unable to access shops or receive deliveries when roads are impassable
  • Clinic and pharmacy workers — needing to receive medicine supplies when delivery networks have broken down
  • Small business owners — dependent on reliable, affordable deliveries to keep trading

We also recognised a secondary need: the delivery riders themselves. When fuel becomes unaffordable, riders lose income entirely. DROPPE creates a new category of work — drone pilots — that doesn't depend on fuel.

"We imagined a grandmother in Ipoh waiting for her blood pressure medicine, and a food stall owner in Puchong who can't afford to keep his fridge stocked. Those are the people DROPPE is built for."

🎯 Define — Framing the problem clearly

After empathising with our users, we wrote a clear problem statement to guide our design:

"People in Malaysian communities need a way to receive essential supplies — food, medicine, emergency kits — that does not depend on petrol, so that they can stay safe and well even when fuel runs out."

We mapped the constraints our solution would have to meet:

  • Must not use petrol — the whole point is fuel independence
  • Must work during floods — Southeast Asia's biggest natural crisis trigger
  • Must be affordable — crisis prices already hurt families; our solution can't add to that
  • Must be legal — has to work within Malaysia's CAAM drone regulations from day one
  • Must be usable — accessible to people who don't own smartphones or speak English

This definition framework ruled out several early ideas (electric scooters still depend on road access; community food banks don't solve remote delivery) and pointed clearly toward autonomous aerial delivery.

💡 Ideate — Generating our ideas

In our ideation session, we brainstormed over 20 possible solutions to the fuel crisis delivery problem. Here are the main alternatives we considered and why we chose drones:

  • Electric cargo bikes — cheaper to run, but still road-dependent and limited by floodwaters
  • Community food banks — helpful, but doesn't solve the delivery problem, especially for medicine
  • Bicycle couriers — fuel-free but physically demanding and slow over longer distances
  • Electric vans — still road-dependent; no advantage during floods or fuel shortages affecting grid power
  • 🏆 Solar drone network — chosen because it bypasses roads entirely, scales across a city, and is genuinely zero-fuel

We chose the drone network because it was the only idea that would work equally well on a clear sunny day and during a flooded road emergency — which is when a fuel crisis in Southeast Asia would be most dangerous.

"The question we kept asking: what still works when everything else breaks down? Drones were the answer every time."

🔧 Prototype — Building the solution

We built two parallel prototypes: a physical product prototype and a digital app prototype.

Physical prototype — the cargo pods: We designed two interchangeable pods that clip onto the same drone airframe. The Food Pod uses insulated walls to maintain temperature. The Emergency Pod carries a pre-packed first-aid kit with medicine, a blanket, water, and a torch. We tested insulation using ice and a food thermometer to verify temperature hold over a simulated 15-minute flight.

Digital prototype — the DROPPE app: We built a fully working HTML/CSS/JavaScript prototype including a live zone-mapped tracker, a working wallet with top-up and transfer, multilingual switching (English, 中文, Bahasa Melayu), and a chatbot assistant. This is not a mockup — it runs in any browser.

"Building something real — not just drawing it — forced us to answer questions we hadn't thought of. Why does the drone need to confirm delivery? What happens if the zone changes mid-flight?"

🧪 Test — Evaluating what works

We tested DROPPE against three key questions:

  • Does the pricing model hold up mathematically? We ran cost calculations for 10 different delivery distances and confirmed the RM3 + RM1.20/km model is consistently cheaper than estimated crisis-era petrol delivery by RM2–5 per order.
  • Does the insulation work? We built a simple Food Pod from cardboard with foam insulation and measured temperature loss over 20 minutes. Hot food held above 55°C for the first 12 minutes — sufficient for a local 1–2 km delivery.
  • Does the app communicate clearly? We asked three classmates unfamiliar with the project to complete an order using only the app. All three completed it successfully without help, validating our user interface design.
"The biggest surprise in testing was the zone map — users loved being able to see the flight path and understand why certain areas were marked caution. It made the safety system feel real."
STEAM Evidence

Five lenses, one solution.

Our STEAM Week brief asked us to show evidence across Science, Technology, Engineering, Arts, and Mathematics. Here's how DROPPE answers each one.

Science

Energy sources, materials & environmental impact

DROPPE's drones run on lithium battery packs recharged at solar Auto-Swap Stations — converting sunlight directly into stored electrical energy, with no combustion and no fossil fuel input at any stage. Electric motors are significantly more energy-efficient than internal combustion engines, since they don't lose energy to idling, gear friction or stop-start traffic.

Renewable energy source: photovoltaic solar panels, not fossil fuel combustion.
Electric motors convert >85% of stored energy into motion, versus ~25–30% for petrol engines.
Direct flight paths avoid the energy losses of traffic congestion, idling, and acceleration.
Zero tailpipe emissions per delivery — no CO₂, no particulate pollution released.
"DROPPE is scientifically suitable because it replaces fuel combustion with direct solar-to-battery energy transfer, cutting carbon emissions and removing dependence on a strained fuel supply chain."

Mathematics

Cost modelling, efficiency data & evidence

The DROPPE app charges a transparent delivery fee of RM3.00 base + RM1.20 per km flown. We compared this against estimated petrol-rider delivery costs during a fuel shortage, where surcharges and rationing typically push prices 40–80% higher.

1.8 km delivery: RM5.16 on DROPPE vs. estimated RM8–11 for a petrol rider during rationing surcharges.
Direct flight paths are roughly 30–40% shorter than road routes, cutting delivery time and energy per trip.

Malaysia avg. tariff ≈RM0.21/kWh; drone uses ~0.15 kWh/km = RM0.032/km energy cost.
These are our team's worked example calculations from the app's real pricing model — not independently verified market data — used to demonstrate the mathematics behind our solution.

Design & Engineering

Prototype, materials & physical testing

Two modular cargo pods sit at the centre of the physical design: a temperature-controlled Food Pod, and an Emergency Pod carrying a real first-aid kit. Both swap onto the same drone airframe, charged at solar Auto-Swap Stations between flights.

Food Pod: insulated foam walls keep hot and cold orders at safe temperatures throughout transit.
Emergency Pod: first-aid kit, fever medicine, rehydration salts, blanket, water and a torch.
Swappable battery packs allow a drone to return to full charge in minutes, not hours — minimal downtime.
Flight envelopes are tested against Malaysia's CAAM drone regulations before any route is approved.
Our biggest design trade-off: keeping the Food Pod fully insulated while staying light enough for efficient flight. We solved it with recycled foam padding — cheap, lightweight, and effective.
The Drone

Features of the DROPPE delivery drone.

Every feature was chosen to work reliably in Malaysia's tropical climate, within CAAM regulations, and under crisis conditions.

Solar-rechargeable lithium battery

Charges at Auto-Swap Stations in under 25 minutes. Swappable for zero downtime between deliveries.

GPS-guided precision landing

Lands within 1 metre of the target location using real-time GPS. No pilot required on-site.

Weatherproofing for tropical conditions

IP54-rated shell handles Malaysia's heavy rainfall, heat and humidity. Designed for 300+ flying days per year.

Modular cargo pod system

Swaps between Food Pod (insulated, temperature-controlled) and Emergency Pod (medical supplies) in seconds.

CAAM-compliant flight envelope

Operates under Malaysia's Civil Aviation Authority rules: below 120m, within Safe Zones, with automatic No-Fly avoidance.

Live telemetry to the app

Speed, altitude, battery level and ETA are broadcast to the customer's phone in real time during every flight.

Team Reflection

What we learnt building DROPPE.

Our honest reflection

DROPPE started as a single question: what keeps food and medicine moving when petrol runs out? We expected to find a simple answer. Instead, we discovered how deeply interconnected modern supply chains are — and how fragile they become when one resource disappears.

The hardest part of this project was not the technology. Drone delivery works. Solar charging works. The hardest part was designing for people who had never heard of either — the grandmother waiting for medicine, the food stall owner who doesn't own a smartphone. It forced us to think about usability, language, and trust in ways we hadn't expected.

"Building a working app taught us more than any presentation could. When something breaks, you have to understand it deeply enough to fix it. That forced us to actually understand the maths, the physics, and the design choices — not just describe them."

If we were to continue this project beyond STEAM Week, our next steps would be applying for a CAAM experimental drone permit, partnering with a local solar company to model the real cost of a charging station, and running a genuine user test with residents in Puchong.

We are proud of what we built. We are also clear about what it still is: a well-researched prototype, not yet a product. The gap between the two is what real engineering looks like — and we now have a much clearer picture of how to close it.
